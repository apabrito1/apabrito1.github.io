My notes on several Bend-and-Break lemmas.

This is a document that I am preparing as I read about various bend-and-break theorems. Let us fix the notational convention that we are going to use in these notes. All schemes are of finite type over a field $k.$ By a variety we mean a separated, integral scheme of finite type over a field. A curve is a 1-dimensional connceted, reduced proper scheme over a field $k$, not necessarily irreducible. By a curve on a variety $X$ we mean a nonconstant morphism from a curve with smooth irreducible components to $X$, or equivalently its image in $X$. 

\vspace{4mm}

First let me mention a few things about morphisms that contract curves. By the famous Stein Factorization theorem, any proper morphism $f:X\to Y$ between varieties can be factorised as $X \xrightarrow{f'} Y'\xrightarrow{g} Y$ where $f'$ has connected fibers and $g$ is a finite morphism. Actually having connected fibers is a consequence of the fact that in the proof of Stein Factorisation we take $Y'=\textbf{Spec}(f_*\mathcal{O}_X)$ which implies that $\mathcal{O}_{Y'}\cong f'_*\mathcal{O}_X.$ It is important to note that the curves contracted by $f$ and $f'$ are the same.

\vspace{2mm}

\textbf{Remark:}   If $f$ has connected fibers to begin with then $g$ is bijective. Let $k$ be a field of characteris $0.$ Then by generic smoothness theorem, there exists a dense open subset $V\subseteq Y$ such that $g|_{g^{-1}(V)}:g^{-1}(V)\to V$ is smooth. Since $g|_{g^{-1}(V)}$ is flat and $g$ is bijective, $g|_{g^{-1}(V)}$ is a bijective finite étale morphism, which implies that it is an isomorphism. Hence $g$ is a birational morphism. Moreover if $Y$ is normal then for any open subset $U\subseteq Y$, $\Gamma(g^{-1}(U),\mathcal{O}_{Y'})\cong \Gamma(U,\mathcal{O}_Y)$ since $g$ is finite and $\Gamma(U,\mathcal{O}_Y)$ in its fraction field. Hence $g$ is an isomorphism.
